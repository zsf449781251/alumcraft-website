How to Sublimate on Aluminum Blanks: A Complete Beginner's Guide

April 23, 2026 · 8 min read

Sublimating on aluminum blanks produces some of the sharpest, most vibrant prints in the heat transfer world. The metal surface adds a professional weight and pearlescent sheen that paper and polymer substrates simply can't match. If you're new to sublimation on aluminum — or just want to refine your process — this guide covers everything from equipment to finished product.

What Is Sublimation Printing?

Sublimation is a process where solid dye turns directly into gas under heat and pressure, bonding permanently with a specially coated surface. Unlike inkjet or screen printing, the dye becomes part of the material rather than sitting on top of it. This means the print won't peel, crack, or fade under normal use.

For aluminum blanks, the surface is pre-coated with a polyester-based sublimation layer. When heated to the right temperature, the gas-phase dye molecules penetrate this coating and lock in place — producing photo-quality images with excellent color gamut.

What You'll Need

  • Sublimation aluminum blanks — pre-coated, pearlescent white surface
  • Heat press machine — flat press or swing-away, at least 15×15 inches
  • Sublimation printer — Epson EcoTank or Sawgrass with sublimation ink
  • Sublimation paper — standard weight (100-120gsm)
  • Heat-resistant tape — to hold the transfer in position
  • Teflon sheet or silicone pad — protects the heat press platen
  • Protective gloves — blanks get very hot

Recommended Heat Press Settings

Settings vary by press model and blank thickness, but here's a reliable starting point for 0.55mm aluminum blanks:

Standard Settings for Aluminum Blanks

Temperature: 180–200°C (356–392°F)
Time: 60–90 seconds
Pressure: Medium to firm
Cooling: Let the blank cool before peeling the paper

Always test on a sample blank first. Every heat press runs slightly differently, and dialing in the perfect settings for your specific equipment is worth the extra 5 minutes.

Step-by-Step Process

1. Prepare Your Design

Design in CMYK color mode at 300 DPI. Mirror the image horizontally (flip it) before printing — the transfer will reverse it again during pressing. Size your design to match the blank dimensions exactly, or slightly smaller to leave a clean border.

2. Print the Transfer

Load sublimation paper into your printer. Print on the coated side — it's usually the brighter, smoother side. Let the print dry for a few minutes before handling to prevent smudging.

3. Position and Tape

Place the printed transfer face-down onto the aluminum blank, aligning the design. Use heat-resistant tape on all four sides (or at least two opposite edges) to prevent shifting during pressing.

4. Press

Preheat your press to the target temperature. Place the blank (transfer-side up) on the lower platen. Close the press and apply firm, even pressure for the set time. The pressure should be sufficient to ensure full contact between the transfer paper and the blank surface.

5. Cool and Peel

Open the press and immediately move the blank to a heat-safe surface. Do not peel the paper while the blank is hot — let it cool for 10–15 seconds, then peel at a 45-degree angle in one smooth motion. Peeling too early can cause ghosting or incomplete transfers.

6. Inspect

Check the print for full coverage, vibrant colors, and no artifacts. If colors appear washed out or patchy, increase temperature by 5°C or add 10–15 seconds and try again.

Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them

  • Ghosting (double image): The paper shifted during pressing. Use more tape and ensure even pressure.
  • Washed-out colors: Temperature too low or time too short. Increase by 5–10°C or 15 seconds.
  • Brown or yellowish tones: Temperature too high or time too long. Reduce heat or duration.
  • Uneven transfer: Pressure not even across the surface. Check that the heat press platen is parallel and the blank sits flat.
  • Peeling or scratching: Wrong blank — ensure you're using sublimation-coated aluminum, not raw metal.
  • Square edges visible: Design doesn't fully cover the blank. Print slightly oversized or use a bleed area.
